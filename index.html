The Phoenix Coalition

Preamble of the Party

Welcome to The Phoenix Coalition. Our mission is to build a better future through innovative policies and governance. The Phoenix Coalition believes that lasting freedom comes not from constant upheaval, but from rebuilding with discipline, fairness, and purpose.

After prolonged polarization, economic inequality, and loss of trust in leadership, we refuse to choose between disorder and complacency. Instead, we rise from the ashes of past failures to forge a new path forward. We will lead with strength, enforcing law with consistency, protecting the economy, and respecting every citizen's right to live freely in their personal life.

We believe in a government that is firm yet fair, one that upholds the rule of law while ensuring justice and opportunity for all while staying out of personal choices. The role of the government is to keep order, ensure opportunity, and defend liberty; not to moralize or divide.

We will fight corruption wherever it lives, whether in government offices or boardrooms. We will rebuild trust in our institutions, restore dignity to work, and make sure that safety and stability are rights, not privileges.

The Economy

The United States has grappled with chronic economic instability characterized by inflationary pressures, an unequal distribution of wealth, and unsustainable debt of the federal government. Economic turbulence has deepened the divide between owners of capital and waged workers, making business and worker certainties a thing of the past. Real GDP growth in the United States slowed to 2.5 percent in 2023, according to estimates from the U.S. Bureau of Economic Analysis, while inflation stayed above target levels, eroding the purchasing power of millions of households (Bureau of Economic Analysis). Meanwhile, public debt has climbed beyond $34 trillion, leading to potential long-term risks to national solvency and economic sovereignty (U.S. Department of the Treasury). The economy's current structure rewards speculation over production and short-term profit over sustainable growth. Economic disorder has weakened the very foundations of national stability. When markets are allowed to run amok and there's no coherent government policy, inequality deepens and social trust erodes. According to the Federal Reserve, 64 percent of Americans live paycheck to paycheck, while the top 10 percent of earners now hold nearly 70 percent of household wealth (Board of Governors of the Federal Reserve System). Such disparity undermines economic mobility and public confidence in capitalism itself. Also, persistent inflation and budgetary irresponsibility weaken national defense and infrastructure, forcing dependence on foreign debt holders. A strong economy is not merely a matter of market performance but rather one of resilience, fairness, and accountability. The Phoenix Coalition aims to ensure that a disciplined economy forms the bedrock of free society. It would require that economic power be exercised with the same precision as political power: openly, effectively, and with accountability. The Coalition will work toward a mixed-market system in which private enterprise can flourish under fair regulation, while public institutions play a stabilizing role, never as competitors. The party is opposed to untrammeled corporate concentration and irresponsible government spending alike, seeing either one as threatening national independence. Prosperity must come from productivity and ingenuity, not manipulation. Economic growth should reward work, foster self-reliance, and reinforce long-term national well-being. The Phoenix Coalition stands for fiscal responsibility by balancing budgets, targeted taxation, and responsible investment. Corporate tax loopholes that reward offshore profit-shifting will be closed; small and mid-sized businesses are incentivized through domestic production and apprenticeship programs. According to the Economic Policy Institute, investments in technical training yield higher wage growth and productivity over time compared with broad tax cuts (Economic Policy Institute). The Coalition will reinforce antitrust enforcement to ensure proper competition in key industries: technology, agriculture, and energy. Federal public spending will focus on modernizing infrastructure, developing renewable energy, and providing national supply-chain security. The party attaches federal investment directly to measurable outcomes so that it ensures accountability for every dollar spent. The Coalition also supports monetary stability through coordination between fiscal policy and the Federal Reserve. Excessive stimulus and speculative credit expansion will be avoided in favor of sustainable interest rate management and prudent lending standards. The Coalition maintains that economic freedom is preserved not through deregulation but through balance. Markets must serve the public good, not dominate it. The Phoenix Coalition believes in an economy that rewards discipline and modesty, cultivation instead of spectacle. Financial strength is a matter of national security and moral obligation. With balanced budgets, open markets, and productive investment, the Coalition affirms that stability is not the enemy of prosperity but its foundation.

Foreign Policy

The United States has long stood as a dominant global power, shaping international politics through economic, diplomatic, and military influence. This position has yielded significant benefits in global trade, security, and alliances. However, it has also fostered instability through excessive intervention and the misuse of military strength. Throughout history, the United States has often acted as a self-appointed global arbiter, engaging in conflicts that lie beyond its direct interests. Such actions expand both alliances and hostilities, entangling the nation in disputes that drain resources and credibility. The Phoenix Coalition strongly believes that American foreign policy must shift from reactive dominance to measured strategy. Foreign policy defines the stability and security of the nation. When military and economic resources are overextended, domestic priorities suffer. The Coalition recognizes that unchecked global involvement weakens the very foundation it aims to protect. According to the Congressional Research Service (2023), the United States has engaged militarily in over 90 foreign operations since 1945, often without achieving lasting peace or strategic gain (Congressional Research Service). The Iraq War of 2003 exemplifies this failure: launched on unfounded claims of weapons of mass destruction, the intervention cost over $1.9 trillion and claimed hundreds of thousands of lives, while diminishing American credibility among allies (Crawford). Overextension transforms power into vulnerability. The Coalition’s doctrine will ensure that the United States acts with discipline abroad to maintain strength at home. The Phoenix Coalition will uphold a foreign policy grounded in rationality, restraint, and precision. Military power is essential, but it must be wielded with calculation, not emotion. The Coalition rejects impulsive interventions and advocates for decisions based on verified intelligence, transparent objectives, and defined outcomes. Strategic patience is not weakness; it is the highest form of strength. The Coalition supports maintaining alliances through diplomacy and mutual interest while avoiding dependency or entanglement. Foreign engagement must serve national security and economic stability, not ideology. The Phoenix Coalition will establish a framework of balanced global engagement. Military deployment will require clear objectives, limited scope, and periodic review to prevent prolonged conflicts. National resources will be allocated proportionally between defense, domestic infrastructure, and research, ensuring no single sector undermines another. The Coalition will invest in cyber defense, intelligence coordination, and diplomatic channels that prioritize preemptive stability over reactionary warfare. The Coalition will institutionalize decision-making, evaluating all foreign interventions thoroughly before deployment. We will operate on verified intelligence and cost-benefit analysis, ensuring that no decision is driven by political expediency or public emotion. The Coalition affirms can only be achieved through cooperation, intelligence sharing, and targeted precision; rejecting all broad, indefinite conflicts. The Coalition will hold domestic equilibrium as the foundation of international strength. A nation fragmented at home cannot command respect abroad. Investments in education, technology, and infrastructure will enhance the credibility of American leadership by example rather than force. The Coalition rejects the illusion that constant intervention equals global influence. True power is measured by stability, endurance, and the respect of nations who see the United States as a model of order.

Immigration

The Phoenix Coalition views immigration as both a national strength and a structural responsibility. A stable nation must manage its borders with discipline and its values with consistency. The Coalition rejects the false binary between open borders and closed doors. It recognizes that immigration, when lawful and regulated, strengthens the economy, diversifies innovation, and reinforces our national character of perseverance and enterprise. Yet it also maintains that uncontrolled or inconsistent immigration policy erodes public trust, weakens labor standards, and undermines the rule of law. The Coalition holds that the rule of law is the foundation of fairness. A government that enforces laws selectively encourages both disorder and resentment. Therefore, the coalition supports immigration systems that are transparent, merit-based, and humane. Enforcement must be firm yet efficient, ensuring that those who enter do so legally and that violations of immigration law are swiftly addressed through structured, due-process mechanisms. However, enforcement must also be humane and fair, rejecting cruelty and discrimination as tools of deterrence. The Coalition supports the modernization of our archaic, overwhelmed immigration infrastructure. Such upgrades include digital visa tracking, updated employment verification systems, and increased staffing for immigration courts to reduce backlogs. It also supports a streamlined legal pathway for workers and families who contribute to the economy, satisfying critical labor shortages in agriculture, technology, and healthcare. Studies by the Pew Research Center and the Migration Policy Institute have shown that legal immigrants contribute billions in tax revenue and start businesses at higher rates than native-born citizens (Pew Research Center; Migration Policy Institute). For these reasons, immigration policy must reward contribution and compliance rather than perpetuate inefficiency or fear.

Criminal Justice Reform

The current criminal justice system in the United States is a failing institution that delivers neither safety to citizens nor fairness to its subjects. Two decades into reform, our nation still imprisons more than 2.2 million people, a number inflated by the nearly 10 million admissions processed through county jails every year (Carson; Zeng and Minton). Violence has been in decline since the 1990s, but the Bureau of Justice Statistics estimates that almost two-thirds of released prisoners are rearrested within three years. This is evidence of a system that punishes efficiently yet rehabilitates poorly (Alper and Durose). The imbalance shows that the pursuit of justice has been supplanted by the machinery of bureaucracy, vast in scale and limited in effectiveness. All elements of national stability suffer when a justice system goes awry. Where crime is endemic and enforcement lacks credibility, citizens lose faith in the institutions meant to keep them safe. Researchers estimate that about 40 percent of the incarcerated population is behind bars without a clear justification from a public safety standpoint, costing taxpayers billions without demonstrable evidence of deterrence (The Sentencing Project). The fragmented and slow reporting of national crime data, identified by the Council on Criminal Justice as a key barrier to sound policymaking, prevents timely and targeted responses (Council on Criminal Justice). A rudderless system diminishes both safety and liberty, undermining civic trust at its foundation. The legitimacy of the rule of law depends not on the fervor with which it punishes but on its precision. The Phoenix Coalition believes liberty requires order and order requires accountability. Justice should be predictable, quantifiable, and transparent, strict in defending peace yet restrained in intrusion into private life. The Phoenix Coalition rejects, as dangerous to freedom, both leniency that enables disorder and excess that erodes freedom. The purpose of law is not vengeance; it is equilibrium. A society cannot thrive when it tolerates lawlessness or punishes its own people as an industry. The party supports firm enforcement guided by data, fairness, and public oversight, ensuring that discipline strengthens liberty rather than restricts it. The Phoenix Coalition advocates for a system based on truth, proportionality, and effectiveness. The cornerstone will be reliable data of national crime and sentencing through an unprecedented digital transparency effort that thoroughly tracks sentencing trends, recidivism rates, and enforcement outcomes. Evidence-based policing and judicial policy replace reactive enforcement, focusing resources on violent and repeat offenders rather than low-level, nonviolent cases. Targeted intervention has always been proven to cut violence far more effectively than mass incarceration (National Institute of Justice). Sentencing reform will be based on proportionality and purpose, with mandatory minimums and outdated "three-strikes" laws revised to reflect actual public safety outcomes. With 17 percent of state prisoners incarcerated for nonviolent drug offenses, the need for judicial discretion and rehabilitative alternatives is clear (Carson). Evidence-based reentry programs focused on employment, housing stability, and behavioral accountability will be expanded. Research from the National Institute of Justice shows that such programs can reduce recidivism by up to 20 percent (National Institute of Justice). Finally, transparency will be institutionalized through standardized public reporting of arrest, conviction, and sentencing data. Open access to performance metrics at every stage will ensure oversight, build public confidence, and prevent abuses of power.

Healthcare and Pharmaceutical Regulation

For too long, healthcare in the United States has been treated as a commodity rather than a collective necessity. The Phoenix Coalition rejects this notion. Health is not a product to be bought or sold; it is a tool of national survival. A nation’s longevity and strength depend on the physical and mental endurance of its people. According to the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC), expanding access to healthcare saves more than 212,000 lives in the United States every year. The average American life expectancy stands at approximately 77 years, compared with 68 years in countries with minimal healthcare infrastructure, such as India (Murphy et al.). These differences illustrate a fundamental truth: access to medical care determines whether a population thrives or decays. A healthy population is a productive population. The Coalition views healthcare not as a matter of convenience, but as a foundation of national security and efficiency. Research from the Kaiser Family Foundation (2024) estimates that preventable illness costs the U.S. economy more than $260 billion annually in lost productivity (Kaiser Family Foundation). Illness undermines labor strength, weakens morale, and erodes the resilience of the workforce. Therefore, ensuring universal access to medical care is not merely a moral imperative — it is a strategic necessity. The right to health carries with it a civic duty to maintain that health for the collective good. The Phoenix Coalition guarantees universal medical care as a right of citizenship and a requirement of service. Every citizen, regardless of class or occupation, shall receive the treatment necessary to preserve their ability to perform their societal function. In return, all citizens must comply with regular health evaluations and contribute anonymized data to federal health systems for research and monitoring (Centers for Disease Control and Prevention). Refusal to participate constitutes refusal to serve. Health resources are allocated by strategic priority: individuals essential to defense, production, or research receive priority in treatment and recovery. Medicine is distributed according to necessity, not desire. Rehabilitation is reserved for those who contribute to the collective; idleness does not merit restoration. The Coalition envisions a healthcare system based on universal access, scientific regulation, and moral discipline. All pharmaceuticals and medical substances shall be subject to the oversight of the Food and Drug Administration (FDA). Each drug must pass through the FDA’s 12-step approval process, a safeguard established by the 1938 Federal Food, Drug, and Cosmetic Act to prevent the circulation of unsafe products (U.S. Food and Drug Administration). The unregulated creation or distribution of drugs constitutes a direct act against the nation’s welfare. Medication exists to heal, not to gratify. Pharmaceutical production will occur exclusively under federal supervision. Government laboratories will control dosage, availability, and purpose of all medical products. This regulation will eliminate the epidemic of overdose that claims more than 110,000 American lives annually, according to the National Institute on Drug Abuse (National Institute on Drug Abuse). Recreational drug use is prohibited; chemical dependency weakens individual discipline and collective strength. The 6.8 million Americans who currently use marijuana recreationally represent the erosion of civic resilience. Scientific research links such use to delayed neurological development and dependency among adolescents (Substance Abuse and Mental Health Services Administration). The Coalition stands unequivocally against these indulgences. Scientific advancement remains a national priority. As of June 2025, the United States hosts roughly 159,000 registered clinical trials, with new drug development requiring an average of 10 to 12 years and $2.6 billion to complete. Only 12 percent of candidates achieve approval (U.S. Food and Drug Administration). These figures justify the Coalition’s centralized model of research oversight, ensuring that time, funding, and innovation are directed toward medicine that enhances survival and operational strength; trauma care, radiation treatment, and resistance medicine. Experimental treatments must be sanctioned through federal review to ensure safety and alignment with national objectives. The Coalition rejects medical decadence in all forms. Recreational pharmaceuticals and pain-numbing indulgences divert resources from genuine healing. Globally, 15.8 million aesthetic surgeries were performed in 2023, including 1.6 million in the United States, often with medical complications. In the same year, 14.2 million Americans misused prescription psychotherapeutic drugs, and 8.5 million abused pain relievers (Substance Abuse and Mental Health Services Administration; International Society of Aesthetic Plastic Surgery). Such misuse represents moral and civic decay. The Coalition affirms that medicine exists to restore function, not to gratify vanity. Within this system, the body serves the mission, medicine serves the people, and the people serve the nation. No life will be wasted to negligence, indulgence, or inequity. Health is not a privilege; it is the instrument by which the Coalition ensures survival, progress, and the unbreakable vitality of the United States.

Abortion

The Phoenix Coalition believes that the issue abortion is one of personal autonomy, responsibility, and moral order. While the Coalition recognizes the importance of safeguarding individual rights and personal choices, it also believes that laws should provide clear, predictable boundaries that protect the health of both individuals and society. The Coalition’s stance on abortion is grounded in the principle that personal freedom must be protected, but personal responsibility should also be acknowledged. The party supports a legal right to abortion within the framework of a regulated, ethical, and transparent system. This ensures that women have access to safe, legal healthcare options while also addressing the complex moral, social, and public health aspects of the issue. Abortion, the Coalition believes, should be available to those who need it, but it should not be viewed as a carefree or unrestricted decision. There must be respect for both the autonomy of the individual and the moral values of society (Guttmacher Institute; Kortsmit et al.). The Coalition supports the right to choose up to a reasonable point in pregnancy, acknowledging that early-stage abortions are a necessary part of protecting individual autonomy and health. However, the Coalition also believes that as a pregnancy progresses, the state has a legitimate interest in regulating the procedure more strictly to balance the health and safety of the mother with the ethical considerations surrounding the potential life of the fetus (Kortsmit et al.). The second trimester, in particular, requires careful consideration of both public health and individual liberty. The Phoenix Coalition opposes unrestricted abortion practices of the past, which allowed for late-term procedures to be performed without stringent medical oversight or clear necessity. However, the Coalition also opposes total bans on abortion, recognizing that such laws would not only harm women’s rights but also force the nation into a regressive, authoritarian stance that undermines both personal freedom and public health (Guttmacher Institute). To ensure balanced and effective regulation, the Coalition advocates for comprehensive sex education and contraceptive access as primary tools in preventing pregnancies, increased support for maternal health including prenatal care and mental health services, a national health policy that allows for a reasonable waiting period and counseling for those seeking an abortion, and ensuring safe and legal procedures for those in need (Centers for Disease Control and Prevention).

Second Amendment Rights

The Second Amendment of the United States Constitution was established to ensure citizens’ protection against tyranny and to safeguard self-defense. Yet in the modern era, this right has been distorted into a source of violence, fear, and disunity. Unrestricted firearm access has fostered mass shootings, civil unrest, and mistrust in authority. According to the Gun Violence Archive (2024), an average of 327 people are shot every day in the United States, and approximately 117 die from gun-related injuries (Gun Violence Archive). The right that once symbolized freedom now fuels chaos. The Phoenix Coalition contends that to preserve national stability, the Second Amendment must be reinterpreted not as an absolute personal liberty but as a regulated civic responsibility bound to the collective security of the state. A nation that arms itself without restraint cannot sustain peace. The proliferation of firearms has made safety a privilege rather than a guarantee, weakening the trust between citizens and their institutions. Studies from the Pew Research Center indicate that over 70 percent of Americans, including many gun owners, support measures such as universal background checks and safe-storage laws (Pew Research Center). The Phoenix Coalition believes firearms ownership should be treated like driving a car or practicing a trade: a lawful liberty subject to training, licensing, and oversight. The party supports constitutional gun ownership for responsible adults while demanding strict measures to keep weapons away from those who pose a clear danger. The Coalition’s Responsible Firearm Ownership framework includes universal background checks on all gun sales, licensing and safety training requirements, limits on high-capacity weapons designed for rapid mass killing, and extreme-risk protection orders that temporarily remove firearms from individuals who present a documented threat to themselves or others (Davis and Packard; Pew Research Center). The Coalition also supports investments in community violence-interruption programs and data-driven policing strategies in neighborhoods most affected by gun violence (National Institute of Justice). Under this disciplined system, the right to bear arms remains protected, but the culture of negligence and impunity surrounding firearms is brought to an end.

Education

The United States emerges from war with an education system that is fractured from decades of inaccessibility, deprivation, and neglect. Under the Capitol, learning was not a right but a privilege granted selectively. It was weaponized to maintain control and was stripped from many districts entirely; approximately 21% of U.S. adults are unable to complete literacy tasks at a level necessary for the modern workforce, and an estimated 130 million adults have low literacy skills (Schmidt; National Center for Education Statistics). The result of inconsistent education is a nation whose citizens lack a solid academic foundation, a vocational skill set, and an understanding of civics. This divided education system has led to a divided people, and division cannot be afforded in the post-war nation. The Coalition believes a stable nation cannot exist without an educated society. National resilience is built on the backbone of literacy, critical thinking, and practical competence. Without these skills, misinformation spreads easily and productivity weakens, which slows our recovery from the war. The Phoenix Coalition understands that the same forces that enabled the dominant to dominate the nation, dependency and a lack of education, threaten long-term stability. Once the curriculum is predictable, accessible, and measurable, liberty and order will be strengthened. A fractured system creates weakness and instability, while a structured system will allow the nation not just to recover but to advance. The Phoenix Coalition asserts that education is a collective obligation: citizens have the duty to learn, while the nation has the responsibility to teach our generation. Our party proposes a national curriculum reform, which would have a unified national standard of education that must be taught nationwide, with emphasis on factual literacy, scientific knowledge, and technical experience rather than arbitrary skill sets. These standards will be essential to national reconstruction. In the U.S., schools with strong career-readiness programs report graduation rates above 90%. This ensures that training is completed (Association for Career and Technical Education). To achieve this, our party will establish a National Teacher Corps to train instructors and professors to consistent standards and deploy them in underserved areas. A nation’s strength depends on its ability to prepare its own citizens for work and civic participation. Adults with low literacy are far less likely to secure stable, living wage employment (Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development). To restore competence and eliminate educational inequity nationwide, the Phoenix Coalition will repurpose existing facilities into nationwide educational facilities. These facilities will offer apprenticeships, technical certifications, and community service programs. A centralized monitoring system will track literacy rates and workplace readiness and ensure that resources are distributed evenly throughout (National Center for Education Statistics). Although education is a right, citizens are expected to participate in and complete this program, and the state will guarantee access to learning materials and meaningful education. The Phoenix Coalition stands against the inequity of knowledge, both the government’s weaponization of it and its neglect of those who desperately need it. The Coalition will induce a populace that is both informed about current issues and able to act on that knowledge practically. We will ensure that citizens can meaningfully contribute to our nation, think for themselves, and protect their freedom. Reform in education is what will turn our divided, struggling society into a unified, advancing one.

The Environment

The United States faces increasing environmental threats from rising global temperatures, limited resources, and increasing natural disasters. According to NOAA, 2023 experienced 28 billion-dollar weather events—the most on record (National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration). Meanwhile, carbon emissions keep increasing, and the scarcity of water endangers agricultural and industrial output. These situations become more serious with inconsistent policy and fragmented enforcement where environmental protection is a matter of political cycles and not part of a national strategy. Current practices are uncoordinated, data is imprecise, and there is no long-term focus. Environmental instability threatens not only ecosystems but also economic growth, national security, and public health. According to the World Bank, climate-related disasters cost the United States $165 billion in 2022 alone (World Bank). Such losses burden taxpayers, stretch emergency systems beyond their limits, and reduce agricultural yields. In addition, pollution and unsafe air quality disproportionately affect working-class and rural communities, exacerbating the very inequality the Coalition strives to eradicate. A disordered environment yields disordered governance. If nothing is done at the structural level, the country's ability for energy independence and food security will degrade, thus undermining sovereignty and social stability. For the Phoenix Coalition, environmental policy is a matter of national discipline, not ideological debate. The environment is not a moral battleground but a logistical responsibility. The Coalition disavows either unrestricted exploitation of natural resources or poorly coordinated regulatory overreach. It supports a framework of strategic sustainability in which protecting the environment and achieving industrial development are complementary goals. The management of resources should be informed by science, accountability, and technological innovation—not partisan inclination. A strong environment is, declares the Coalition, the precondition of a strong economy. The Coalition supports a national transition to energy that is reliable, independent, and efficient. Renewable development will be initiated using federal incentives that are measurable with benchmarks for actual carbon reductions. According to the Department of Energy, already 22 percent of the nation's electricity generation comes from renewable sources (U.S. Energy Information Administration). The Coalition will seek to safely double that amount by 2035 without compromising grid stability. Investment in nuclear energy, advanced battery storage, and carbon capture technologies will ensure sustainable energy without economic disruption. To protect natural resources, the Coalition advocates for modernized water infrastructure and stricter enforcement of pollution controls, particularly in manufacturing and agriculture. Federal agencies will coordinate under a single Environmental Data Command to monitor emissions, deforestation, and resource extraction in real time. This precision-based model replaces reactive policy with proactive management. The Coalition will also tie corporate sustainability standards to tax incentives, rewarding measurable environmental responsibility rather than symbolic compliance. The Phoenix Coalition believes that environmental order is inseparable from economic and social order. Preservation without structure leads to inefficiency, while progress without restraint leads to collapse. Guided by science-based regulation, innovation, and long-term planning, the Coalition envisions a nation that treats environmental stewardship as an act of governance, not charity. Sustainability isn't a slogan—it’s a system, one built to endure.

Fictional Issue: The Hunger Games

The Coalition strongly condemns the Hunger Games as an immoral and inefficient use of power that betrays the principles of order, justice, and liberty. The Games represent the corruption of governance, the transformation of law into spectacle and control into cruelty. A government that claims to preserve peace through ritualized violence does not enforce order; it destroys it. The Coalition holds that true stability cannot be achieved through fear. Order must arise from legitimacy, not coercion. The Games eroded both, turning citizens into participants in their own subjugation. Such institutions degrade the moral foundation of our nation and divert resources away from legitimate interests. The Coalition maintains that the preservation of social order must never come at the expense of human dignity. Crime and rebellion are symptoms of imbalance, not excuses for oppression. The Hunger Games, in their waste and barbarity, exemplify the failure of leadership that persists today (Collins).
